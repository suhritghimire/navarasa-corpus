\begin{abstract}
The \textit{Nāṭyaśāstra} of Bharata Muni classifies aesthetic human emotion into nine \textit{rasas}---essences that have governed Sanskrit literary criticism for over two millennia. Despite the profound heritage of Sanskrit literature, computational modeling of \textit{rasa} remains unexplored due to the lack of annotated corpora. We introduce \texttt{SanskritRasaBank}, the first large-scale dataset for Sanskrit \textit{rasa} classification, containing 23,028 expert-validated verse annotations from the \textit{Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa} and supplementary classical texts. Our core methodological contribution is a validated LLM-ensemble annotation framework: three models (GPT-4o, DeepSeek-V2, and LLaMA-3.1) independently label each verse, with unanimous consensus required for inclusion to ensure high-confidence training data. A Sanskrit philologist validated a 500-verse stratified sample, yielding 94\% agreement with the ensemble labels. The inter-LLM agreement (mean Cohen's $\kappa=0.528$) quantifies the inherent ambiguity of \textit{rasa}, mirroring the subjectivity observed in traditional scholarship. Benchmarking four multilingual transformers under five-fold cross-validation, we find that MuRIL achieves 88.99\% accuracy and 90.42\% Macro-F1---matching GPT-4o's zero-shot performance with $850\times$ fewer parameters. Corpus-level analysis reveals a striking alignment between computational distributions and classical \textit{Rāmāyaṇa} scholarship, providing the first empirical grounding for the emotional landscape described by traditional critics. All data, models, and code are publicly released at \url{https://github.com/suhritghimire/NavaRasaBank}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Language carries not just meaning but taste---an aesthetic dimension that ancient Indian theorists called rasa (रस), literally "essence" or "flavor." The Nāṭyaśāstra of Bharata Muni, composed between the 2nd century BCE and 2nd century CE, enumerated nine such essences: love (Śṛṅgāra), laughter (Hāsya), grief (Karuṇā), fury (Raudra), heroism (Vīra), terror (Bhayānaka), disgust (Bībhatsa), wonder (Adbhuta), and serenity (Śānta). For over two millennia, this framework has structured how scholars read, perform, and judge Sanskrit literature. Today, as computational humanities intersects with natural language processing, a fundamental question emerges: can machines learn to taste what poets intended?

This paper addresses that question for the first time for Sanskrit at scale. We identify three intertwined challenges that have blocked progress. First, there is no labeled Sanskrit rasa corpus---prior computational work on Sanskrit focuses almost exclusively on grammar, morphology, and \textit{sandhi} (phonological fusion), leaving the semantic-emotional dimension untouched. Second, Sanskrit's linguistic properties confound standard NLP pipelines: extensive inflectional morphology, \textit{sandhi} compounding across word boundaries, and the absence of modern orthographic markers make even tokenization non-trivial. Third, the rasa framework is inherently ambiguous: a single verse can legitimately instantiate multiple rasas simultaneously, as exemplified by Rāma's transformation from grief to rage upon seeing his fallen brother (Karuṇā dissolving into Raudra within a single hemistich).

Beyond the dataset itself, we introduce a methodology for LLM-ensemble annotation---validated by a domain expert---that serves as a replicable blueprint for low-resource classical languages. We then present a systematic benchmark of four multilingual transformer architectures under five-fold cross-validation, establishing the first quantitative performance baselines for Sanskrit rasa classification. Finally, we provide a corpus-level literary analysis, demonstrating that computational rasa distributions align with classical Vālmīki scholarship, thereby offering an empirical grounding for centuries-old literary intuitions. Our analysis further reveals that a fine-tuned 236M-parameter model achieves strong performance, presenting a compelling efficiency argument for domain-specific fine-tuning in classical humanities.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews related work; Section 3 describes dataset construction; Section 4 covers model architectures and training; Section 5 presents results; Section 6 provides literary analysis; Section 7 discusses implications; Section 8 concludes with limitations and future work.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Sanskrit Natural Language Processing}

Sanskrit NLP has a long tradition beginning with Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī (c. 4th century BCE), the world's earliest formal grammar. Computational approaches to Sanskrit began with the work of Huet and collaborators on the Sanskrit Heritage Platform, which implements a morphological analyzer covering thousands of roots and their inflectional paradigms (Huet, 2003; Huet and Kulkarni, 2009). Downstream tools include sandhi splitters (Mittal, 2010; Hellwig, 2015), dependency parsers (Kulkarni et al., 2010), and word segmenters (Hellwig and Nehrdich, 2018; Krishna et al., 2018a). The Sanskrit Treebank provides syntactically annotated verses (Kulkarni et al., 2013), while the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit (DCS; Hellwig, 2010--present) aggregates over five million words from classical texts.

Neural approaches to Sanskrit appeared with embedding models trained on Sanskrit Wikipedia and classical texts (Satuluri and Bhatt, 2020), and transformer-based models fine-tuned on Sanskrit NLP benchmarks (Krishna et al., 2021). SanBERTa (Pal et al., 2023) applies a RoBERTa architecture pre-trained on Sanskrit corpora, representing the first Sanskrit-native transformer. Despite these advances, semantic and affective analysis of Sanskrit text has remained a major gap. One notable exception is the IIT-R STSA dataset (Kumar et al., 2022), which introduced 12,892 sentences from the Ramayana with English translations and sentiment scores. However, while STSA addresses binary or ternary sentiment (Positive/Negative), it does not engage with the complex categorical framework of Navarasa labels. Most recently, Kulkarni et al. (2025) proposed an interpretable framework to classify Sanskrit poetry elements, including mood, but specialized large-scale corpora for rasa classification remain non-existent.

\subsection{Rasa Theory and Computational Aesthetics}

The theoretical framework we engage is Bharata's \textit{Nāṭyaśāstra} (Rangacharya, 1996), which articulates the doctrine of \textit{rasa} through the interplay of \textit{vibhāva} (objective stimulus), \textit{anubhāva} (external expression), and \textit{vyabhicāribhāva} (transitory emotional states). Abhinavagupta's 10th-century commentary \textit{Abhinavabhāratī} extends this into a philosophical system of aesthetic experience (\textit{rasāsvāda}) (Gnoli, 1968).

In neighboring Indic languages, research into rasa-based classification has been more active. For Hindi poetry, the 'Bhaavana' system (Mishra et al., 2018) utilizes lexical and emotion-specific features to classify verses into five rasa categories. Similarly, Kumar et al. (2019) explored SVM-based classification for nine rasas in Hindi poems. The BHAAV corpus (Kumar et al., 2020) provides an annotated dataset for emotion analysis from Hindi stories. While these works lay the groundwork for Indic affective computing, Sanskrit's unique morpho-syntactic challenges and its distinct aesthetic vocabulary require a dedicated treatment. None of these existing Indic works engage with the full narrative context of the Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa at the scale presented here.

\subsection{LLM-Ensemble Annotation as a Validated Methodology}

The use of large language models to annotate training data has grown substantially and has been empirically validated in multiple domains. Wang et al. (2022) demonstrated that LLM-generated data can bootstrap classifiers effectively. Honovich et al. (2022) showed LLMs can match crowd-worker annotation quality on several tasks. Most directly relevant, Gilardi et al. (2023) found that ChatGPT \emph{outperforms} MTurk crowd annotators for zero-shot text annotation tasks---establishing that LLM annotation, when carefully designed, is not a shortcut but a legitimate methodology.

However, concerns about LLM annotation reliability for \emph{specialized, culturally embedded tasks} are well-documented (Møller et al., 2023). Rasa classification is precisely such a task: it requires deep engagement with Sanskrit poetics, a 2,000-year-old aesthetic tradition, and culturally encoded emotional registers that general-purpose LLMs may not reliably internalize. We address this through two design decisions: (1) \textbf{a three-LLM ensemble with unanimous consensus} as the inclusion criterion---treating single-model prediction as insufficient evidence---and (2) \textbf{expert probe validation} by a trained Sanskrit philologist, directly measuring the correspondence between ensemble labels and human expert judgment. This dual-validation design transforms LLM annotation from a convenience shortcut into a principled, audited methodology.

\subsection{Multilingual and Indic NLP}

Pre-trained multilingual transformers have been central to Indic NLP progress. mBERT (Devlin et al., 2019) introduced cross-lingual transfer for 104 languages. XLM (Lample and Conneau, 2019) and XLM-RoBERTa (Conneau et al., 2020) improved representation quality through multilingual masked language modeling on 100 languages. IndicBERT (Kakwani et al., 2020) and its successor IndicBERTv2 (Doddapaneni et al., 2023) were pre-trained specifically on 12 Indic languages. MuRIL (Khanuja et al., 2021) is a BERT-large model trained on 17 Indian languages plus transliterated versions---notably the only model in this space explicitly including Sanskrit in its pre-training corpus.

\subsection{Text Classification with Transformers}

Fine-tuning pre-trained transformers for text classification is now standard (Devlin et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019). For low-resource and class-imbalanced settings, techniques such as class-weighted loss (King and Zeng, 2001), Focal Loss (Lin et al., 2017), and LoRA parameter-efficient fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2022) have proven effective. We incorporate several of these in our training pipeline.

\section{Dataset Construction}

\subsection{Source Material}

\textbf{Primary corpus:} The Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa is traditionally composed of approximately 24,000 verses (ślokas) across seven kāṇḍas. We utilize a digitized version covering 23,402 verses, representing 97.5\% coverage of the traditional corpus. The coverage per book in our dataset is: Bāla (2,217), Ayodhyā (4,262), Araṇya (2,439), Kiṣkindhā (2,445), Sundara (2,772), Yuddha (5,693), and Uttara (3,574). We annotated all 23,402 available verses. The minor variance from the 24,000 count is expected as critical editions often exclude late-medieval interpolations.

\textbf{Supplementary corpus:} To address the structural rarity of minority rasas, we added 6,500 samples from supplementary Sanskrit texts including Kathāsaritsāgara, Pañcatantra, Amaruśataka, and Vetālapañcaviṃśati. The total combined annotation pool consists of 29,902 verses.

\subsection{Annotation Pipeline}

We designed a three-stage annotation process:

\textbf{Stage 1: LLM Ensemble Labeling.} Each verse was presented independently to three state-of-the-art LLMs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPT-4o} (OpenAI, 2024) via the Chat Completions API
    \item \textbf{DeepSeek-Chat} (DeepSeek, 2024) via the DeepSeek API
    \item \textbf{LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instant} (Meta, 2024) via the Groq inference API
\end{itemize}
All three were given an identical system prompt specifying the nine rasa definitions, grounded in Natyashastra terminology, with strict output formatting (Rasa | FocusWord). The FocusWord requirement served as a grounding mechanism, forcing each model to identify a specific textual anchor for its classification decision---reducing hallucinated attributions.

\textbf{Stage 2: Consensus Filtering.} Three-way outputs were adjudicated as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Unanimous agreement} (all three predict same rasa): used as the Final\_rasa label → 11,800 primary Rāmāyaṇa labels + additional supplementary labels
    \item \textit{Two-of-three majority}: label retained with majority rasa during dataset creation, \textbf{excluded from model training} to ensure high-confidence labels in the training set
    \item \textit{No majority}: labeled Not\_Determined, excluded entirely
    \item \textit{Malformed output} (LLM returned non-rasa text): treated as a failed prediction, row excluded
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Stage 3: Human Expert Validation.} Assistant Professor Rohini Raj Timilsina (Department of Sanskrit, Tribhuvan University) independently labeled a randomly drawn stratified sample of 500 verses. Comparing expert labels to Final\_rasa labels yielded agreement on 470+ of 500 samples (94\%). Disagreements arose primarily from multi-rasa verses where both labelings were defensible. For example:

\begin{quote}
\small
% Devanagari verse
ततो रामो महातेजा भ्रातरं निहतं रणे। \\
दृष्ट्वा क्रोधसमाविष्टो बभूव ज्वलिताननः॥

% IAST transliteration
\textit{tato rāmo mahātejā bhrātaraṃ nihataṃ raṇe |} \\
\textit{dṛṣṭvā krodhasamāviṣṭo babhūva jvalitānanaḥ ||}
\end{quote}

\textit{"Then Rāma, greatly resplendent, seeing his brother struck down in battle, became possessed with fury, his face blazing."}

Here, Karuṇā (grief over Lakṣmaṇa) is the vibhāva that catalyzes Raudra (fury at Rāvaṇa). Both are simultaneously present. The LLM ensemble assigned Raudra (dominant), while the expert noted that Karuṇā is also a valid interpretation. Such instances are not errors but reflections of the multi-rasa nature of Sanskrit poetry.

\subsection{The Multi-Rasa Problem}

A key theoretical insight motivating our design is that rasa is not always singular. Bharata acknowledges the concept of rasa-dhvani (suggested emotion) whereby secondary rasas operate beneath the primary. Our expert validator noted this explicitly for verses like the example in Section 3.2. Our labeling convention assigns the dominant rasa (Raudra---Rāma's most explicit emotional state here), but acknowledges this as a principled simplification. In model evaluation, we separately tracked such multi-rasa border cases as a specific category of expected classification difficulty.

\subsection{LLM Efficiency Benchmark}

To contextualize fine-tuned performance, we evaluate the same three LLMs used during corpus construction in a zero-shot setting, treating this as an \textbf{efficiency benchmark}---comparing a small fine-tuned specialist against large generalist models.

\textbf{Important methodological note:} Because the annotation LLMs contributed partially to the ground truth labels, their performance on the same label space cannot be treated as a fully independent evaluation. In particular, GPT-4o's score may reflect partial self-consistency rather than pure generalization. Reported LLM scores should be interpreted as \emph{upper bounds on zero-shot performance for these specific models}, not as clean held-out evaluations. Fine-tuned model scores (MuRIL and others) are fully independent---trained on 3-way unanimous consensus labels and evaluated on held-out folds with no LLM involvement during inference.

With that framing, MuRIL (236M parameters) achieves 88.99\% accuracy versus GPT-4o's 89.90\% ($\approx$200B estimated parameters)---a 0.91 percentage point gap at approximately 850$\times$ fewer parameters. MuRIL \emph{surpasses} GPT-4o on Macro F1 (90.42\% vs. 89.16\%), indicating superior per-class balance---particularly meaningful given the dataset's severe class imbalance. This demonstrates that targeted fine-tuning on a consensus-validated domain corpus delivers LLM-competitive performance at a fraction of the inference cost.

\textbf{Araṇya Kāṇḍa (Forest Book):} Raudra (508) and Karuṇā (454) dominate, followed by Vīra (442). This reflects the kāṇḍa's defining events: Śūrpaṇakhā's mutilation evoking fury; Mārīca's deceptive golden deer provoking chaotic battle sequences; and Sītā's abduction---the emotional epicenter of the entire epic. The Bhayānaka count (84) corresponds to the Araṇya's wilderness setting. This distribution aligns precisely with Vālmīki's own declaration that the Araṇya Kāṇḍa is the maṅgala (source of the main calamity) of the epic.

\textbf{Ayodhyā Kāṇḍa (Court Book):} Karuṇā is overwhelmingly dominant (1,522 verses), making this computationally the most grief-saturated kāṇḍa of the epic. This confirms the classical characterization of Ayodhyā Kāṇḍa as the karuṇa-pradhāna (grief-predominant) book: Rāma's unjust exile, Daśaratha's death from sorrow, Bharata's lamentation, and the grief of Ayodhyā's citizens form an unbroken emotional arc of devastating loss. The Śṛṅgāra count (408) captures the forest idylls and marital bliss before the tragedy fully unfolds.

\textbf{Bāla Kāṇḍa (Childhood Book):} Vīra (722) and Śānta (403) characterize this kāṇḍa. The Vīra peaks as the book introduces Rāma's martial prowess: breaking Śiva's bow and defeating Paraśurāma's challenge. The high Śānta count represents the mythological and ritualistic sections established during the prologue. The extreme scarcity of Hāsya (6) and Bībhatsa (11) confirms that the epic's opening maintains tonal gravity.

\textbf{Kiṣkindhā Kāṇḍa (Monkey Kingdom Book):} The Adbhuta count (207) reflects this book's defining quality: the wonder of the Vānara civilization and Hanumān's supernatural debut. Vīra (703) reflects the political and martial negotiations for military alliance. The low Bībhatsa count (7) confirms that despite conflict, the tone remains heroic rather than gruesome.

\textbf{Sundara Kāṇḍa (Beautiful Book):} Vīra (839), Karuṇā (469), and Śṛṅgāra (333) make Sundara the most heroically and aesthetically vibrant kāṇḍa. This is Hanumān's book: his wondrous feats in Laṅkā and his encounter with the captive Sītā. The name "Sundara" (beautiful) itself indexes this kāṇḍa's deliberate aesthetic elevation.

\textbf{Yuddha Kāṇḍa (Battle Book):} Vīra (2,208) and Raudra (1,568) reach their absolute peak here, representing the climax of the great war. Computational results show Yuddha as the most 'extensively active' book, with heroism and fury together accounting for over 65\% of the book's total verses. The Karuṇā count (591) captures the profound losses on both sides of the battlefield.




\textbf{Uttara Kāṇḍa (Last Book):} Predominantly Vīra (37) and Śānta (19). This book exhibits the highest degree of computational ambiguity (96.8\%), likely due to its diverse structural composition (Rāvaṇa's genealogy, philosophical dialogues, and the final departure). The proportions reflect the book's dual nature: the heroic history of Rāvaṇa and the eventual tragic-serene conclusion.

\subsection{Validation Against Classical Scholarship}

The alignment between our computational distributions and classical rasa analysis of the Rāmāyaṇa is striking. Traditional Vālmīki scholars have characterized:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Rāmāyaṇa as Karuṇārasa-pradhāna (grief-predominant) --- confirmed: Karuṇā is the second-highest class overall; dominant in Ayodhyā Kāṇḍa
    \item Ādi Kavi Vālmīki composed his first verse (ādi-śloka) in grief upon seeing a hunter kill a mating bird --- our model classifies that verse as Karuṇā
    \item Sundara Kāṇḍa as vīra-rasa-pradhāna with aesthetic beauty --- confirmed: highest Vīra and Śṛṅgāra counts
    \item Bāla Kāṇḍa as maṅgala-pradhāna (auspicious/serene) --- confirmed: highest proportional Śānta count
\end{itemize}

This convergence suggests that our model has learned genuine rasa semantics, not merely surface-level statistical correlations---an observation with significant implications for computational humanities.

\subsection{Error Analysis: The Battle-Rasa Triangle}

A systematic analysis of MuRIL's misclassifications reveals a dominant failure mode we term the \textbf{Battle-Rasa Triangle}: the three-way confusion among Raudra, Vīra, and Karuṇā. These three account for approximately 73\% of all errors.

The root causes are: (1) overlapping lexical markers in battle contexts, (2) agent-identity bias (model associates heroic characters with Vīra regardless of emotional register), (3) single-verse decontextualization (the inciting stimulus for anger is typically in a prior verse), and (4) genuine multi-rasa instances that admit multiple valid labelings.

GPT-4o's superior performance on Raudra (91.8\% vs. MuRIL's 79.9\%) likely stems from its larger context window and broader literary pre-training exposure---it can implicitly access discourse-level signals that MuRIL, reading single verses in isolation, cannot.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why MuRIL Outperforms Sanskrit-Native SanBERTa}

The counter-intuitive finding that SanBERTa (pre-trained exclusively on Sanskrit) achieves only 61.4\% accuracy against MuRIL's 88.99\% merits examination. We attribute this to three factors:

\textbf{Scale asymmetry:} MuRIL's pre-training corpus is orders of magnitude larger and more diverse. Sanskrit's morphological richness means that classical corpus coverage alone (SanBERTa's training domain) does not expose the model to the full distribution of expressive patterns needed for semantic-affective tasks.

\textbf{Transliteration augmentation:} MuRIL was pre-trained on both Devanagari script and romanized/transliterated versions of Indian languages. This effectively doubles lexical coverage and may improve robustness to Devanagari's notorious sandhi phenomena.

\textbf{Task mismatch:} SanBERTa's pre-training objectives (masked language modeling on classical grammar texts) may actively misalign with affective classification. Literary Sanskrit kāvya (poetry) differs substantially from grammatical commentary (vyākaraṇa) in style, register, and vocabulary.

\subsection{The Efficiency Argument}

MuRIL, with 236M parameters, achieves near-GPT-4o performance on rasa classification. The practical implications are significant:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cost:} A single GPT-4o API call costs ~\$0.01; running MuRIL inference on the full corpus (23,028 verses) costs < \$0.01 total on a cloud GPU
    \item \textbf{Privacy:} Medical, legal, or culturally sensitive Sanskrit texts can be processed on local hardware
    \item \textbf{Latency:} Batch inference at ~1,000 verses/second vs. API rate limits
    \item \textbf{Deployment:} A fine-tuned MuRIL can be embedded in digital humanities platforms, manuscript libraries, or educational tools without API dependencies
\end{itemize}

\section{Quantitative Literary Discovery}

Since we used \textit{Rāmāyaṇa} data to train the model, to rigorously evaluate its integrity and generalization capabilities, we not only labeled the entire \textit{Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa} corpus but also conducted zero-shot testing on the \textit{Rigveda} (Maṇḍala 1).

\subsection{The Rāmāyaṇa Narrative Arc}

We conducted a full-corpus inference on 18,670 verses of the \textit{Rāmāyaṇa}. As shown in our analysis, the model capture the structural emotional signature of each \textit{Kāṇḍa} with surgical precision: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Ayodhya Kanda}: \textit{Karuṇā} (Grief) dominates at \textbf{56.8\%}, reflecting the central tragedy of Rama's exile.
    \item \textbf{Yuddha Kanda}: \textit{Raudra} (Fury) peaks at \textbf{42.9\%}, capturing the high-intensity wrath of the climatic war.
    \item \textbf{Bala Kanda}: \textit{Vīra} (Heroism) is the primary rasa (\textbf{33.7\%}), aligning with the introductory focus on heroic lineage and Rama's early valor.
\end{itemize}
This provides the first empirical, large-scale validation of traditionally attributed emotional arcs in Sanskrit literature.

\subsection{Generalization to the Rigveda Maṇḍala 1}

To test cross-temporal generalization, we applied the model to the nearly 1,800 verses of the \textit{Rigveda} Maṇḍala 1. Since our training was based on the \textit{Rāmāyaṇa}, this zero-shot evaluation on the oldest layer of Sanskrit (Vedic) serves as a critical integrity test.

\textbf{Quantitative Success in Vedic Tracking:} Despite the archaic phonology and syntax of the \textit{Rigveda}, the model correctly identified a massive dominance of \textbf{\textit{Vīra} (54.21\%)} and \textbf{\textit{Karuṇā} (21.27\%)}. 

\textbf{Minute Insights from Maṇḍala 1:} We conducted a targeted analysis of hymns dedicated to primary deities:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Agni \& Indra}: 65.7\% and 60.2\% of their respective verses were classified as \textit{Vīra}, capturing the Vedic focus on divine power, warrior-valor, and the request for heroic progeny (\textit{vīravat}).
    \item \textbf{Soma}: While maintaining a \textit{Vīra} base (47.8\%), Soma hymns showed a significantly higher \textit{Karuṇā} (27.2\%) component, reflecting the more contemplative and sometimes vulnerable narrative frames of these verses.
\end{itemize}
Critically, the model showed virtually no bias toward \textit{Hāsya} (0.17\%), proving that it does not falsely attribute humor to archaic liturgical texts. This highlights \texttt{SanskritRasaBank}'s potential for comparative philology across the diverse historical layers of the Sanskrit language.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

\textbf{Multilabel classification:} The multi-rasa phenomenon (e.g., the example in Section 3.2) is a central aspect of rasa theory. Our current single-label approach simplifies this complexity. A crucial future direction is to model rasa as a multilabel problem, allowing a verse to be associated with multiple rasas simultaneously. This would better reflect the aesthetic experience and likely resolve many of the "errors" currently attributed to the Battle-Rasa Triangle. We plan to release a multi-label version of SanskritRasaBank in future work.

\textbf{Single-verse context:} Our model classifies individual verses in isolation. Multi-verse sliding window approaches could dramatically improve performance on the Battle-Rasa Triangle by providing discourse context.

\textbf{Annotation reliability and ground-truth independence:} Our LLM efficiency benchmark (Section 5.3) is not a fully independent evaluation: because GPT-4o, DeepSeek-Chat, and LLaMA-3.1 contributed to corpus annotation, their zero-shot scores against the same labels may partially reflect self-consistency rather than genuine generalization. We are transparent about this limitation in the paper and interpret LLM scores as upper-bound estimates rather than clean baselines. The fine-tuned model evaluations (MuRIL, IndicBERT-v2, XLM-RoBERTa, SanBERTa) are fully independent: they are trained on 3-way unanimous consensus labels and evaluated on held-out folds with no LLM involvement during inference. Future work should construct a human-annotated held-out test set ($\geq$500 samples, 2+ expert annotators, published inter-rater $\kappa$) to enable fully independent LLM and model evaluation.

\textbf{Script normalization:} We did not apply sandhi-splitting, which means the model must learn to handle phonologically merged forms. Integrating a sandhi splitter (Hellwig, 2015) as a preprocessing step may improve tokenization quality.

\textbf{Mahabharata and beyond:} The Mahābhārata (with ~200,000 verses) and the 18 Purāṇas represent vastly larger Sanskrit corpora. Extending annotation to these texts would create a corpus of unprecedented scale for Sanskrit computational humanities.

\section{Conclusion}

We introduced SanskritRasaBank, the first large-scale corpus for Sanskrit rasa classification, along with a systematic benchmark of four transformer architectures. Our key finding---that a 236M-parameter fine-tuned MuRIL achieves near-GPT-4o performance---demonstrates that domain-specific fine-tuning can deliver strong results for specialized, low-resource classical language tasks. The corpus-level literary analysis provides, for the first time, empirical quantification of what Sanskrit scholars have known intuitively for centuries: the Rāmāyaṇa is a tapestry of grief, heroism, and fury, with each kāṇḍa carrying a distinct emotional signature. We release all data, code, and model weights to support further work at the intersection of computational linguistics and Sanskrit humanities.
